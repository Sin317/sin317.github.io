My First Adventure with Horse Riding#

Stepping into the world of horseback riding can feel intimidating, especially if your experience with horses extends no further than petting one at a county fair. That’s exactly where I found myself last weekend when I decided to take the plunge with Equine Escapes, a family owned horse ranch nestled in the WA.

Kelly, the owner arrived at the meetup point and I was blown away by the carriage - it was a huge one with 6 horses. We also saw lots of other riders with their horses. It was a sunny day so everyone wanted to enjoy it seems!

Meeting the Herd#

The six horses that would guide our journey through the wilderness each possessed distinct personalities that quickly became apparent:

  1. Dakota, the undisputed alpha male of the herd, carried himself with quiet confidence and stoic dignity.
  2. Diego radiated excitement, his curious eyes constantly scanning the surroundings, seemingly thrilled about the adventure ahead.
  3. Jack, my eventual companion, was an Arabian male with a gentle temperament and an insatiable appetite for any patch of grass within reach.
  4. Teddy, a gigantic Belgian horse, commanded attention with his majestic presence and powerful frame.
  5. Trinity, aptly named “the princess,” carried herself with elegance as the second-in-command of this equine community.
  6. Tinker, a sweet-natured female, moved with a mellow grace that instantly put newcomers at ease.

It took about 40 minutes to stretch out the horses and get them into the bridle and saddle a few of them up.

I was paired with Jack, positioned third in our single-file formation behind Diego and Dakota. My initial challenge became immediately apparent—Jack’s irresistible urge to sample every patch of grass along the trail required gentle but firm guidance. Through this unexpected test, I gradually mastered what I came to think of as the “gentle approach to discipline,” learning to communicate with my four-legged partner through subtle cues.

To my surprise, the ride proved remarkably comfortable. Despite warnings about soreness that typically plagues first-time riders, my improving posture seemed to align naturally with Jack’s trotting rhythm along the trail.

Our path wound through a lush forest tapestry—dense trees, vibrant undergrowth, and occasional steep inclines that showcased the horses’ sure-footedness. Midway through our journey, we paused for photographs, capturing moments that would later translate the majesty of both the wilderness and our equine companions.

Horse communication 101#

The journey also offered an unexpected education in equine communication. The occasional loud flatulence and rhythmic breathing were, as I learned, the horses’ way of signaling their well-being to the rest of the herd. Our guide explained that continuous huffing would indicate distress—a vital piece of knowledge for any aspiring rider.

Perhaps most fascinating was discovering how responsive these intelligent animals were to human positioning. A gentle squeeze of the calf against their sides, accompanied by a soft verbal cue, would set them in motion—a dance of mutual understanding between species.

The hour-long adventure passed with surprising swiftness, leaving me with muscles that recognized their new experience but also with a profound appreciation for these magnificent animals and the wilderness we had traversed together.
